TalentTap

RoleProduct Designer — end-to-end
Timeline2025
PlatformDesktop web app + Chrome extension over LinkedIn
TeamClient-side product team — PMs and developers

01 — Problem & Solution

Problem

Recruiters source candidates inside LinkedIn, but everything they do with those candidates happens somewhere else. Profiles get copied into spreadsheets, conversations live in email, and the judgement about whether someone fits is spread across all three — so the work of assessing a candidate is mostly the work of moving their data around.

Solution

TalentTap pulls candidate data out of LinkedIn through a Chrome extension and lands it in one workspace where job postings, AI resume analysis, pipelines and messaging sit together. The extension keeps the recruiter on the page they source from; the web app is where the pipeline is actually worked.

02 — Process

01Dashboard

Designed the recruitment overview: pipeline metrics, activity over time, and recent candidate movement, so a hiring manager can read the state of open roles without opening any of them.

02Job postings

A list view of active listings with status and candidate counts, built around quick actions rather than deep navigation — the posting is a container you drop into, not a document you edit.

03Candidates management

The full lifecycle of a candidate record: list, profile, status change, notification states, and the delete confirmation. Designed the states that usually get skipped, because a pipeline is mostly made of them.

04Messaging

Conversation list, expanded thread and compose state, so a message to a candidate does not require a second tool and a second copy of their details.

03 — Key decisions

The tool works inside LinkedIn instead of pulling the recruiter out of it

Problem

Sourcing happens on LinkedIn. Any tool that requires leaving the page to record a candidate competes with the recruiter's own momentum, and loses.

Decision

The extension sits as an overlay on LinkedIn in two states: a compact sign-in, and an expanded panel showing matched candidates with their resumes, match scores and immediate actions.

Why

The capture step has to cost almost nothing or it does not happen. The trade-off is severe constraint: everything essential has to fit into a panel floating over someone else's interface, which is why the expanded state carries only what supports a decision.

AI output is material for comparison, not a verdict

Problem

A scoring model can produce a number for every candidate. A number invites the recruiter to stop reading — and to inherit whatever the model got wrong.

Decision

Analysis results are shown as strengths and gaps that can be scanned and compared, with an explicit loading state and an explicit empty state rather than a silent one.

Why

The decision stays with the person. The screen is designed to be read side by side across candidates instead of collapsing into a single ranking — which costs the recruiter more attention than a verdict would.

Matches are surfaced against a posting, not against the whole database

Problem

A candidate is never good in the abstract, only good for something. A global list of "strong candidates" is not actionable.

Decision

Matching is scoped to a job posting, with colour-coded indicators and the ability to move a candidate into the pipeline from the same screen.

Why

It keeps the comparison honest — everyone on screen is being judged against the same role — and it makes the next action available where the judgement is made.

04 — Outcome

—In production.

—The full recruitment workflow covered end to end, from posting a role to deciding on a candidate, across both the web app and the extension.

—The extension and the web app share one interface language, so the same candidate record reads the same in both places.

05 — What I'd do differently

This project ran without user research. The workflow was reconstructed from the brief and from discussion with the PMs and developers, and the riskiest assumption in it — that recruiters want to stay inside LinkedIn rather than move to a dedicated tool — is exactly the kind of thing that should have been watched rather than assumed. Given the same project again, I'd sit with recruiters during a sourcing session before designing the extension, and I'd test whether the AI analysis screen actually gets read or quietly reduced to its score.

Magnolia

RoleUX/UI Designer — extending an existing product
Timeline2024
PlatformCross-platform: mobile for field workers, desktop for supervisors and admins
TeamClient-side product team — PMs and developers

01 — Problem & Solution

Problem

Park maintenance is documented in the field, by people standing next to a tree, and then has to become a record someone can search, assign and audit from an office. Paper inspection forms make the first half easy and the second half impossible, and the product that replaced them had to serve two groups whose working conditions have almost nothing in common.

Solution

Magnolia covers the whole reporting lifecycle in one system: an interactive map of park objects, structured surveys completed on site, tasks tied to specific locations, and detail pages that hold everything known about an object. I joined an existing product and designed the areas it was missing — authentication, the map experience, garden and object pages, surveys, tasks, and customer and user management — as full mobile and desktop counterparts.

02 — Process

01Authentication and onboarding

Sign-up, login, phone verification, and the forgot-and-reset password journey, for both mobile and desktop, in the product's existing visual language.

02Garden and object detail pages

Every park object carries general information, description, a photo gallery, reports, comments, a QR code and contacts. Structured as a scrollable screen on mobile and a laid-out page on desktop, so that everything known about an object is available without any of it dominating.

03Survey and reporting forms

Multi-step forms with more than fourteen structured fields — species, condition, dimensions, treatment, photos, signature — ordered so they can be completed standing up, outdoors, on a phone.

04Task management

A task list with status, a detail page tied to its map location, a closed state, and the return to the map after completion — because a finished task in the field means moving to the next place, not to the next row.

03 — Key decisions

The map is the way into the product, not a feature inside it

Problem

A park is a place. Finding "the third oak on the east path" through nested menus asks the worker to translate where they are standing into a hierarchy someone else invented.

Decision

The map is the main surface, with states for the default view, a selected garden, a pinned object and fire alert mode, and a list of locations alongside it. Drilling into a garden happens on the map; on desktop it holds the main content area with a collapsible detail panel, on mobile it moves into list and detail views.

Why

Navigation matches how the work actually happens — spatially. The cost is that the map takes the most valuable screen space, which is why the location list sits next to it for anyone who would rather read than pan.

Two roles, two real interfaces, not one squeezed layout

Problem

The field worker and the office manager use the same data under opposite conditions: one is outdoors on a phone with one hand free, the other is at a desk comparing many records.

Decision

Every mobile flow was rebuilt as a desktop equivalent rather than reflowed into one — including the admin areas the field worker never sees, like customers, adding a customer and creating users.

Why

A responsive squeeze would have made one of the two audiences a second-class user of their own tool. The cost is roughly twice the surface to design and maintain, held together by a shared visual language so both groups still recognise the same product.

04 — Outcome

—The work shipped into an existing product rather than as a standalone design.

—Mobile and desktop delivered screen by screen in Figma, including edge cases, empty states, alerts and modals.

—QR codes on object pages give a physical entry point into the system: scan the tag on a tree, land on its record.

05 — What I'd do differently

There was no user research on my part of this project — no time in the field with the people filling in the surveys. That is the gap I would close first, because a fourteen-field form is a very different object on a desk and in the rain, and the only way to know whether the order and the required fields are right is to watch someone complete one on site. I'd also want to see how often the map is actually used to navigate versus the location list, since that assumption shaped the whole entry point.

NexFit

RoleProduct Designer — from scratch, in Figma Make
Timeline2025
PlatformMobile app template · dark UI
TeamDirect with the client, a coach based in the US

01 — Problem & Solution

Problem

A personal trainer who wants their own branded app has two options: pay for a custom product they cannot afford, or use a generic platform that makes them look like everyone else on it. Neither gives them a product that is recognisably theirs.

Solution

NexFit is a complete fitness app designed as a template a coach can buy, rebrand and sell as their own: onboarding that builds a personal plan, training programmes, nutrition and recipes, goal tracking, and a community feed. It is designed so that swapping colours, photography and content produces a different-looking product rather than a reskinned one.

02 — Process

01Home

Split into Today, Recipes and Available Programs, plus settings and individual recipe and active-programme pages, so the app has somewhere to send a user on day one.

02Workout

Programme browsing through to an active session with notes and history — the state a user is in most often, designed as the state, not as a detail page.

03Nutrition

Macro tracking with a calorie breakdown, meal logging, and a recipe library split into Saved and Popular, with full ingredient and preparation pages.

04Goal

Weight progress against a target date, shown as a graph with current and goal figures, so the reason for the daily work stays visible.

05Community

A feed where users post, follow others in the same programme and respond, adding accountability to a product that otherwise only talks to one person.

03 — Key decisions

One question per screen in onboarding, even though it makes the flow long

Problem

A personalised plan needs a lot of input: fitness level, current and goal weight, frequency, workout type, location, goal type, target date. Asked all at once, that is a form nobody finishes.

Decision

Each screen asks one thing and shows progress, ending in a plan summary with a graph that makes the answers visibly worth giving.

Why

Length is the cheaper cost. A long flow with obvious momentum finishes more often than a short flow that looks like paperwork — and the payoff screen is what turns the input into something the user feels they own.

Designed as a template, which changes what a good decision looks like

Problem

Everything in a template will be replaced by someone else: colours, photography, copy, programme content. A design that depends on its specific content breaks the moment a coach makes it theirs.

Decision

Components and layouts are built to survive rebranding — structure carries the product, the dark UI with a teal accent is a default rather than a fixture.

Why

The client is not the end user here; the coach is, and their need is a product that stops looking like mine. That means deliberately leaving room instead of filling it — fewer authored flourishes, more neutrality.

04 — Outcome

—Not released. The product is still in development, now without me.

—More than thirty screens delivered, covering onboarding, workout, nutrition, goals and community.

—Built as a rebrandable template, so one file is meant to become several coaches' apps rather than one product.

05 — What I'd do differently

This project had research behind it, but not the kind that tests a template: nothing validated whether a coach can actually make this app feel like theirs with the levers I left them. That is what I would look at first — put the file in front of two or three coaches, watch them rebrand it, and see what they reach for that isn't there. I'd also want the onboarding tested for length, since the decision to ask one question at a time is a bet that momentum beats brevity, and that bet is measurable.

Insurance Management System

RoleProduct Designer — end-to-end
Timeline2023 — 2025
PlatformWeb platform + mobile app · Hebrew & English
TeamPM · developers · QA, reviewing in Figma

01 — Problem & Solution

Problem

An insurance agent's portfolio is scattered across a dozen insurer systems. Monthly commission reports arrive as separate, SMS-code-protected downloads per insurer, and agents rebuilt the whole picture by hand in Excel — so the data they sold against was often weeks out of date without them knowing it.

Solution

A platform built around what an agent actually does: get the data in, trust it, act on it. A download center pulls monthly reports from every insurer and handles verification codes; the customers lobby ranks the whole book by profitability; and freshness is flagged per policy, so the agent always knows exactly what they can rely on.

02 — Process

01Screen map

Mapped the product's four domains — portfolio, policies and sales, data intake, account — and how they connect, before designing any screens.

02Core user flow

Detailed the monthly loop that everything else serves: download insurer reports, populate the portfolio, scan the customers lobby, open a customer, and act on data that is up to date.

03Roles and access

Onboarding branches by product type — agency, agency plus agent, agent portfolio — each with per-insurer users, permissions and report scope defined up front.

04Design system

A bilingual component system built to survive four combinations at once: Hebrew and English, RTL and LTR, agent and agency views.

05Responsive breakpoints

Data-dense tables reworked per breakpoint — columns collapse into grouped summaries rather than shrinking until they're unreadable.

03 — Key decisions

Mobile is one job, not the whole product

Problem

The analytics workspace can't compress into a phone — but the monthly download ritual, with its per-insurer SMS codes, happens exactly when agents are away from a desk.

Decision

The mobile app is scoped to the download center alone: report status, downloads and SMS verification. Analysis stays on desktop.

Why

Scoping mobile to the moment of need made it genuinely useful — a cramped copy of the dashboard would have served nobody.

The download center shows the process, not just the result

Problem

Reports arrive per insurer, per user, gated by verification codes. When the process was silent, agents couldn't tell a stalled download from a slow one — and blamed the data.

Decision

Every insurer, user and report exposes its state — queued, downloading, verified, failed — with progress and the next action always visible.

Why

Trust in the numbers starts with seeing how they got in. A visible pipeline turned "the system is broken" into "one insurer needs a code".

Staleness is flagged per policy, not per page

Problem

A portfolio is never uniformly fresh — one insurer's report can run a month behind while the rest are current. A page-level warning either cried wolf or got ignored.

Decision

Each policy row carries its own freshness state, and the warning names the exact report to download to fix it.

Why

Agents act per policy, so the warning has to live where the decision is made — and point straight at the remedy.

04 — Outcome

—Released to production and in daily use by agents and agencies.

—One portfolio replaced a dozen insurer logins and manual Excel merges.

—Data trust became a visible system state instead of a guess.

—The bilingual design system held across Hebrew, English, RTL and LTR without forking screens.

05 — What I'd do differently

I would prototype the SMS verification flow with real agents in the first month. It looked like a technical detail, but it is the single moment where the whole system earns or loses trust — and we only understood its weight after the download center was already built. I would also define the RTL/LTR mirroring rules in the design system from day one instead of retrofitting them screen by screen.

Traderise

RoleProduct Designer — end-to-end
Timeline2024 – 2025
PlatformResponsive web app — desktop, tablet, mobile
Team6 people — client, PM, developers reviewing in Figma

01 — Problem & Solution

Problem

Traders had good charting tools and good messaging tools — but not the same tool. Analysis happened in a terminal; the conversation about it happened in a group chat somewhere else, and every idea that moved between the two became a flat screenshot: impossible to zoom, retime or build on.

Solution

Traderise puts the chart and the conversation about it on one screen. Rooms are scoped to a single trading pair, chat shares the trading screen, and analysis is shared as a live, reopenable object instead of a picture — so ideas compound instead of dying in a feed of images.

02 — Process

01Information architecture

Defined the room as the core container of the product — everything a trader needs sits inside it rather than across separate sections.

02Screen map

Mapped every screen and overlay in the desktop product and how they connect, before designing any of them.

03User flows — core journey

Detailed the core journey — finding or creating a room, analysing the chart, and publishing the analysis back into the room.

04User flows — members and permissions

Mapped the separate administrative flow for members, ranks and permissions.

05Design system

Built a component library with variants and states — buttons, inputs, search, filters, list rows, avatars, dialogs — so the interface stayed consistent as scope grew.

06Responsive design

Reworked the product across three breakpoints, rather than scaling the desktop layout down.

03 — Key decisions

The chat lives inside the trading screen

Problem

A trader who leaves the chart to read the discussion loses the thing being discussed. But a chat panel takes width away from a chart that needs every pixel it can get.

Decision

The right-hand panel switches between watchlist and chat, so both are one click away without leaving the chart. On top of that, recent messages surface as a floating card over the chart itself — visible without opening anything.

Why

The chart is the reason people are here; the conversation is the reason they stay. Neither could be made secondary. The floating card was the compromise: it keeps the discussion in peripheral vision at the cost of covering part of the canvas — which is why it can be dismissed at any moment.

An idea is shared as an object, not a screenshot

Problem

The standard way to share chart analysis is to screenshot it. The moment you do, the analysis stops being usable — nobody can change the timeframe, follow a level, or build on what you drew.

Decision

Share draft posts the markup into the chat as a live object. Anyone in the room can open it on their own chart and rework it through Reanalyze.

Why

This is the feature the product exists for. If a shared idea can be reopened and modified, the room becomes a place where analysis compounds instead of a feed of images.

A room is a trading pair, not a topic

Problem

In general-purpose chats, discussion of every instrument runs together. Finding the conversation about the pair you're actually trading means scrolling past everything else.

Decision

Each room is tied to one coin pair. Opening a room loads that chart; the conversation inside it is about that chart and nothing else.

Why

Context shouldn't have to be re-established in every message. When everyone in the room is looking at the same thing, the discussion can start at the substance.

04 — Outcome

→Released to production.

→A design system that held — variants and states covered the interface as scope expanded across rooms, chat, member management and three breakpoints.

→A shared vocabulary with engineering — developers reviewed and commented directly in Figma, keeping design decisions and implementation questions in one place.

05 — What I’d do differently

This project ran without user research. The decisions were made from the product brief and from discussion with the client and the team, and the biggest of them — putting the chat inside the trading screen — is exactly the kind of trade-off that should have been tested rather than argued. Given the same project again, I’d validate the panel-versus-overlay compromise with a handful of traders before committing to it, and I’d instrument the share draft flow so we could see whether shared ideas were actually being reopened and reworked, or just read.
